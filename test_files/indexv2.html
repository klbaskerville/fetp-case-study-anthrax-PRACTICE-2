Introduction to One Health

What is One Health?

One Health is a collaborative, multisectoral, and transdisciplinary approach ‚Äî working at the local, regional, national, and global levels ‚Äî with the goal of achieving optimal health outcomes recognizing the interconnection between people, animals, plants, and their shared environment.

Why One Health Matters for Zoonotic Diseases

  • Approximately 60% of infectious diseases in humans are zoonotic
  • Approximately 70% of emerging infectious diseases are zoonotic
  • Early detection in animals can prevent transmission to humans
  • Control in animal populations may be most effective prevention strategy

Part A: Background and Initial Investigation

üìÖ Friday, April 20, 2018

Seven people presented to a health centre in Kween District, Uganda with swollen skin blisters and lesions with black tissue in the centre.

  • 6 males (ages 14-62), 1 female (age 3)
  • All from Kaplobotwo village (subcounty Ngenge, Kween District)
  • All involved in skinning, butchering, carrying or eating a cow that died suddenly on April 11

Question 1: Would you call this an outbreak? Why or why not?

Key Discussion Points:
  • Yes, this is an outbreak - multiple cases of similar illness in same time and place
  • Cases exceed normal expected frequency (baseline = 0)
  • Common source exposure (the dead cow)
  • Cluster in time (9 days) and place (same village)
  • Similar clinical presentation across cases

Setting: Kween District

üìç Location: Mountainous area, part of Uganda's 'cattle-keeping corridor', bordering Kenya

Community Characteristics:

  • Population: ~100,000 people in Kween district
  • Demographics: >50% under 18 years; ~33% illiterate
  • Livelihood: 85% subsistence farming; 80% raise livestock
  • Communication: ~50% own radios (main info source); <2% own TVs
  • Kaplobotwo Village: 234 residents (127 males, 138 <18 years old)

One Health Coordination Challenges

Question 19: Considering a One Health approach, what challenges can limit effective multisectoral collaboration?

Challenges to Multisectoral Collaboration:
  • Structural/Organizational:
    • Different ministries/agencies with separate chains of command
    • Separate budgets and funding streams
    • Different reporting requirements and timelines
    • Lack of formal coordination mechanisms
  • Technical:
    • Different case definitions and surveillance systems
    • Separate laboratory networks and testing protocols
    • Different data management systems (incompatible)
    • Varying levels of technical expertise
  • Communication:
    • No established communication channels between sectors
    • Different professional languages/jargon
    • Information silos - data not shared
  • Professional/Cultural:
    • Sector-specific priorities and mandates
    • Professional territoriality
    • Lack of understanding of other sectors' roles
  • Resource:
    • Limited funding for joint activities
    • Competition for resources
    • Inadequate staff for multisectoral work
  • Policy/Legal:
    • Lack of legal framework for collaboration
    • Unclear roles and responsibilities
    • No accountability mechanisms for joint action

Part D: Conclusion

Key Lessons from This Outbreak

This outbreak of anthrax in animals and people in the 'cattle-keeping corridor' of Uganda highlights the need for coordinated investigations and response by public and animal health authorities for suspected zoonotic disease outbreaks.

Success Factors:

  • One Health Approach: Multidisciplinary team including human health, animal health, and environmental health professionals
  • Early Intervention: All human cases linked to single cow death on April 11
  • Effective Response: No human cases linked to subsequent cow deaths (April 22, 23, 29) - likely due to:
    • Community health education campaigns
    • Appropriate carcass management
    • Vaccination of susceptible animals
    • Environmental decontamination

Moving Forward: Kween District One Health Taskforce

Based on lessons learned, the outbreak team formed a permanent Kween District One Health Taskforce to:

  • Improve information sharing between animal and human health sectors
  • Support prevention, detection, and response to zoonotic disease outbreaks
  • Build sustainable multisectoral collaboration
  • Strengthen surveillance systems
  • Coordinate community engagement efforts

Appendix: Anthrax Fact Sheet

What is Anthrax?

Anthrax is an illness caused by the bacteria Bacillus anthracis. The bacteria are endemic in soil in certain areas, where they persist as environmentally-resistant spores.

Key Epidemiological Features:

  • NOT contagious: No significant spread between live animals or humans
  • Source: Soil-borne spores in endemic areas
  • Spore resistance: Highly resistant, viable for many years in some soil types
  • Destruction: Spores destroyed by moist heat at 100-105¬∞C for 20 minutes

Anthrax in Animals

Cattle, Sheep, Goats, Camelids:

  • Generally causes sudden death
  • Blood may be present around nose, mouth, anus
  • High levels of septicemia at death
  • Carcasses contaminate environment with spores
  • ‚ö†Ô∏è DO NOT OPEN CARCASS - exposure to oxygen allows spore formation

Pigs:

  • Some natural resistance
  • May recover from disease
  • Signs: bloody feces, nasal hemorrhage, respiratory distress, or mild fever with swelling

Proper Carcass Management:

  • Isolate carcass from other animals
  • Do not open the carcass
  • Decontaminate death site
  • Incinerate or deep burial of carcass

Anthrax in Humans

1. Cutaneous Anthrax (Most Common)

  • Route: Spores enter through skin lesions while handling contaminated animal products
  • Incubation: 1-7 days (can be longer)
  • Signs:
    • Group of blisters with itching and swelling
    • Progresses to painless sore with necrotic black scab (eschar)
    • Typically on hands, arms, face, neck
    • May not seek care due to lack of pain

2. Gastrointestinal Anthrax

  • Route: Eating raw or undercooked meat from infected animals
  • Incubation: 3-7 days (can be longer)
  • Signs:
    • Fever, swollen neck glands
    • Sore throat, headache
    • Nausea and vomiting (may include blood)
    • Diarrhea (may include blood)
    • Abdominal swelling, fainting

3. Inhalational (Pulmonary) Anthrax

  • Route: Inhaling spores (e.g., processing contaminated hides, wool)
  • Incubation: 1-7 days (can be longer)
  • Signs:
    • Fever, fatigue, aches
    • Nausea and vomiting
    • Chest discomfort
    • Cough and difficulty breathing

Other Forms:

  • Meningeal anthrax: Often secondary to other forms
  • Injection anthrax: Associated with contaminated needles

Step 7: Develop hypotheses

After performing descriptive epidemiology (time, place, person), the investigation team should develop hypotheses about the source, mode of transmission, and exposures that caused the outbreak.

Question 5: From a public health perspective, which steps might be approached differently in a zoonotic disease investigation?

Zoonotic Disease Investigation Differences:
  • Step 1 - Team: Must include animal health professionals (veterinarians, animal epidemiologists)
  • Step 3 - Verify diagnosis: Need to verify in BOTH humans and animals; may require different laboratories
  • Step 4 - Case definition: Need separate case definitions for humans AND animals
  • Step 5 - Case finding: Active surveillance in both human and animal populations
  • Step 6 - Descriptive epi: Parallel analysis of human and animal cases; look for temporal and spatial relationships
  • Step 7 - Hypotheses: Consider animal-to-human transmission pathways
  • Step 11 - Control measures: Must address BOTH animal and human populations; prevent environmental contamination
  • Step 13 - Communication: Coordinate between human and animal health authorities; engage agricultural and veterinary sectors

Verifying Diagnosis and Defining Cases

Diagnostic Verification:

Samples from all seven patients' skin lesions were collected and sent to the National Reference Laboratory in Kampala for urgent PCR testing for anthrax.

Question 6: How does an outbreak case definition differ from a standard surveillance case definition?

Outbreak Case Definition vs. Surveillance Definition:
  • Time specificity: Outbreak definitions include specific time period related to outbreak; surveillance is ongoing
  • Place specificity: Outbreak definitions restricted to affected geographic area; surveillance covers broader area
  • Person specificity: May include specific exposures or risk factors related to outbreak source
  • Sensitivity: Outbreak definitions often more sensitive (broader) to capture all related cases
  • Purpose: Outbreak definition aims to identify all outbreak-related cases; surveillance definition aims for consistent long-term monitoring

Case Definitions Developed for This Outbreak

Human Anthrax Case Definitions:

Suspected Cutaneous Anthrax: Onset of itching, reddening or swelling of skin areas AND any of: skin lesions (papule, vesicle, eschar) or lymphadenopathy

Suspected Gastrointestinal Anthrax: Onset of abdominal pain AND any of: diarrhea, vomiting, lymphadenopathy, pharyngitis, or oropharyngeal lesions

Confirmed Anthrax: A suspected case with PCR-positivity for Bacillus anthracis from clinical sample

Time: Onset from April 6 onwards

Place/Person: Person residing in Kaplobotwo village, Kween District

Cattle Anthrax Case Definitions:

Suspected: Sudden death, or death within 24 hours of illness onset, with or without blood-stained discharges from external orifices, in cattle in Kaplobotwo village from April 6 onwards

Confirmed: A suspected cattle case with Bacillus anthracis identified in clinical sample by culture, PCR, or microscopic examination

Active Case Finding

Question 7: What are some ways you might look for additional cases (active case finding) among humans?

Active Case Finding Methods:
  • House-to-house visits in affected village
  • Interview community leaders to identify sick persons
  • Train community health workers to identify and report cases
  • Review health facility records for similar presentations
  • Interview known cases about other sick contacts
  • Trace persons who had contact with the dead cow (skinning, butchering, carrying, eating)
  • Trace meat sold to neighboring villages
  • Community meetings to raise awareness and encourage reporting
  • Radio announcements (given radio is main communication source)

Question 8: What are the advantages of using a disease-specific case investigation form?

Advantages of Disease-Specific Case Investigation Forms:
  • Standardization: Ensures same information collected from all cases
  • Completeness: Reminds investigators to ask all relevant questions
  • Efficiency: Speeds up interviews with structured format
  • Data quality: Reduces errors and omissions
  • Analysis: Makes data entry and analysis easier
  • Training: Helps train community health workers on what to ask
  • Comparability: Allows comparison with other outbreaks
  • Documentation: Creates consistent records for reporting

What Actually Happened:

  • 5 additional cases identified through community leaders
  • 12 suspected cases interviewed using case investigation form
  • Community health workers trained on active case finding and prevention education
  • Visual aids used to educate villagers about anthrax prevention

The Animal Health Investigation

Veterinary Response Actions:

  • Decontaminated cow's death site and butchering site using formalin (10%)
  • Vaccinated all livestock with access to contaminated sites
  • Advised owner on managing anthrax risk
  • Reported to neighboring livestock owners
  • Vaccinated all at-risk livestock
  • Asked owners to monitor animals and report sudden deaths
  • Reviewed district veterinary records for previous 4 months

Question 9: How might information from the animal investigation help the human investigation (and vice versa)?

Animal Investigation ‚Üí Human Investigation:
  • Identifies source of human exposure (specific animals)
  • Timing of animal deaths helps predict human case onset
  • Location of animal cases indicates high-risk human exposure areas
  • Historical animal cases show endemic areas and seasonality
  • Laboratory confirmation in animals supports human diagnosis
  • Animal movements indicate potential spread to other communities
Human Investigation ‚Üí Animal Investigation:
  • Human exposure history identifies unreported animal deaths
  • Human cases may alert veterinary services to animal outbreak
  • Meat distribution patterns help trace infected animals
  • Human symptoms/timing confirm virulence and transmission routes
  • Geographic distribution of human cases helps target animal surveillance

Question 16: Why might some livestock owners with sick animals not come forward during an outbreak? How can reporting and compliance be improved?

Barriers to Reporting:
  • Economic: Fear of loss of livelihood; animals are valuable assets; compensation unavailable
  • Food security: Temptation to salvage meat from dead animals
  • Fear of penalties: Restrictions, quarantine, forced culling
  • Stigma: Social consequences of having disease in herd
  • Lack of trust: In government authorities
  • Knowledge gaps: Not recognizing disease or understanding risks
Improving Reporting & Compliance:
  • Compensation schemes: For reported animals/losses
  • Subsidized vaccination: Make prevention affordable
  • Education campaigns: On disease risks and proper carcass management
  • Community engagement: Involve local leaders and veterinarians
  • Rapid response: Demonstrate benefits of early reporting
  • Non-punitive approach: Support rather than penalize
  • Alternative livelihoods: During outbreaks/quarantines

Part C: Response and Control Measures

April 30 Meeting: Key Findings Summary

  • Human cases started April 13, 2018 from Kaplobotwo village
  • 48 total cases (45 suspected, 3 confirmed)
  • Most likely cause: Anthrax (based on clinical, epidemiological, and lab findings)
  • First cattle case: April 11, 2018
  • 10 cattle suspected/confirmed to have died of anthrax during investigation
  • Retrospective: >150 suspected cattle cases January 1 - April 30 in Ngenge subcounty
  • Livestock NOT routinely vaccinated

Question 17: Based on the investigation findings, what short-term and longer-term actions and control measures should be undertaken?

Short-term Control Measures:
  • Human Health:
    • Treat all cases with appropriate antibiotics
    • Continue active case finding
    • Community education on symptoms and when to seek care
    • Address health facility fears from Marburg outbreak
  • Animal Health:
    • Ring vaccination of at-risk livestock
    • Proper carcass disposal (no opening, deep burial or incineration)
    • Site decontamination with formalin
    • Restrict animal movements from affected areas
  • Food Safety:
    • Ban slaughter/sale of sick animals
    • Education on proper meat cooking (boiling >60 minutes)
    • Trace and recall contaminated meat
Long-term Control Measures:
  • Routine livestock vaccination programs (government subsidized)
  • Surveillance systems for animal and human anthrax
  • Compensation schemes for reported/culled animals
  • Community education on zoonotic disease risks
  • Improved coordination between human and animal health sectors (One Health approach)
  • Training for veterinary and health workers on anthrax recognition
  • Environmental management of endemic areas

Question 18: What are some possible cultural and contextual challenges associated with control measures?

Cultural and Contextual Challenges:
  • Economic dependence: 85% subsistence farming; cattle are livelihood - difficult to ban slaughter of sick animals
  • Food security: Meat from dead animals may be seen as preventing waste in resource-poor setting
  • Vaccination costs: No government subsidy; families must pay; group ownership complicates decisions
  • Traditional practices: Long-standing butchering and meat preparation customs
  • Health facility fears: Recent Marburg outbreak created stigma and fear (quarantine, disinfection, death)
  • Limited education: ~33% illiterate - need appropriate communication strategies
  • Communication challenges: Radio main source (50%); need culturally appropriate messages
  • Limited infrastructure: Road access issues limit rapid response
  • Cross-border issues: Kenya border - animals/people move across
  • Young population: >50% under 18 - may not make household decisions
